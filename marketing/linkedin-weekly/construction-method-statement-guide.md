## Post 1 — Value / listicle (attach construction-method-statement-guide-listicle.png)

The number one reason a method statement gets rejected by a principal contractor?

It is generic. Copied from a different job, with the project name swapped, describing work that is not the work actually happening on site.

Here is what a proper, task-specific method statement should cover:

→ Scope and site access (what it covers, and what it does not)
→ A numbered, step-by-step sequence of operations
→ Plant, people, competence and PPE
→ Hazards and controls that line up with your risk assessment
→ Emergency and rescue arrangements
→ A briefing and sign-off record

The sequence is the heart of it. If the crew cannot follow it step by step, the briefing falls apart, and the briefing is the whole point.

## Post 2 — Hook / time-saver (attach construction-method-statement-guide-hook.png)

Writing method statements from scratch is one of the most time-consuming admin jobs in construction.

So most firms do the obvious thing: they copy an old one and swap the details. That is exactly the habit that gets RAMS rejected, or worse, briefed to a crew when it does not describe the real task.

Here is a faster, safer workflow:

1. Give AI the task, site, plant, crew and key hazards (bullet points are fine)
2. Use a structured prompt that asks for every standard section
3. Get a clear, numbered, task-specific draft back
4. A competent person reviews it, makes it site-specific, then briefs and submits

Half a day becomes 15 minutes. The judgement stays with you, where it belongs.
