### Suggested Responses to Common Questions

**Q: "Why is it free?"**
```
Two reasons: 1) The construction industry needs accessible AI resources — putting it behind a paywall defeats the purpose when adoption is already low. 2) We have a separate premium product (an AI copilot tool) for teams who want more. BuildCopilot is the educational layer that helps people understand what's possible.
```

**Q: "What makes this different from just Googling AI prompts?"**
```
Generic AI prompts don't work well for construction. If you ask ChatGPT to "write a risk assessment," you get something vague. Our workflows are built by people who understand construction — they include industry-specific context, the right terminology, and critically, what to CHECK before you use the output. That review step is what most AI resources skip.
```

**Q: "Do people in construction actually use AI?"**
```
More than you'd think, but mostly quietly. Project managers are using it to draft reports. QS teams are using it to speed up tender analysis. The challenge is there's no structured approach — everyone's figuring it out alone. That's the gap we're filling.
```

**Q: "How did you build this?"**
```
Next.js + Supabase for the platform. The content itself is the real work — every workflow has been written and tested against real construction tasks. We focused on making the prompts genuinely useful rather than building complex tech.
```

### Post Body
```
Hi HN,

I built BuildCopilot (https://buildcopilot.ai) — a free library of AI workflows specifically designed for construction professionals.

The problem: Construction is one of the least digitised industries globally. Most AI resources are built for tech workers, marketers, or developers. Construction professionals (quantity surveyors, project managers, site managers) are left with generic "use ChatGPT" advice that doesn't translate to their specific tasks.

What BuildCopilot does: Provides 50 step-by-step AI workflows for common construction tasks. Each workflow includes the specific prompt to use, which AI tool works best, and — critically — what to verify before trusting the output.

Example workflow: "Tender Analysis for Quantity Surveyors"
- Input: Paste tender return summaries
- Prompt: Structured prompt that asks AI to compare pricing, flag anomalies, identify missing items
- Output: Draft comparison table with commentary
- Review checklist: 5 specific things to verify before using
- Time: ~20 mins vs ~3-4 hours manually

Tech stack: Next.js 16, Supabase, Tailwind CSS v4, deployed on Vercel. All content is server-rendered for SEO. Using programmatic SEO patterns to scale to hundreds of pages.

Covers 12 construction roles, 20 tasks, 10 AI tools reviewed, and 10 detailed guides.

No login, no paywall, no tracking. Just useful content.

Would appreciate feedback, especially from anyone working in construction or other "non-tech" industries where AI adoption is slow.
```

### Suggested HN Comment Responses

**If someone asks about monetisation:**
```
The workflows library is free and will stay free. We have a separate product (AI Construction Copilot) that's a more hands-on tool for construction teams — think of BuildCopilot as the educational/resource layer. The logic is: help people understand what AI can do for their work (free), then offer a tool for those who want to go deeper (paid).
```

**If someone questions whether construction workers use AI:**
```
Fair question. Anecdotally, adoption is growing fast among office-based construction roles — QS, estimators, project managers. These are people spending 3-4 hours writing reports that AI can draft in minutes. Site-based roles are slower to adopt (limited desk time, different workflow). We're starting where the immediate ROI is clearest.
```

**If someone suggests this could be for any industry:**
```
You're right that the pattern is transferable — and that's probably where this goes long-term. But starting narrow is intentional. Generic AI resources already exist. The value is in the domain-specific prompts and review checklists. A construction risk assessment has different requirements than a software risk assessment. The specificity is the product.
```
